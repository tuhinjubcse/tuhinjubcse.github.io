# Evaluation Report: Author Style Transfer via LoRA Finetuning

## Overview

This report evaluates whether LoRA-finetuned MPT-30B-Instruct models can generate text that matches a target author's writing style while maintaining generation quality. We compare **finetuned** (base + LoRA) against **baseline** (base only) models across two authors and two generation tasks.

- **Authors**: Jason Reynolds, Stewart O'Nan
- **Tasks**: Continuation (write next paragraph given context), Summary (write paragraph from plot description)
- **Generation config**: temperature=0.8, top_p=0.9
- **Held-out test books**: *Ghost* (Jason Reynolds), *Last Night at the Lobster* (Stewart O'Nan)

---

## Evaluation Pipeline

### Step 1: Precompute Author Style Embeddings

Using OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small`, we compute embeddings for all training paragraphs of each author (from `split_json/`, excluding the held-out test book). The mean embedding serves as the author's style representation.

### Step 2: Style Evaluation (`eval_style.py`)

Three metrics are computed:

1. **Style Perplexity** -- Perplexity of the held-out test book paragraphs under the model (base or base+LoRA). Lower perplexity means the model better predicts the author's actual writing. This measures how well the model has internalized the author's language patterns.

2. **Linguistic Alignment** -- Adapted from the [StyleTunedLM](https://github.com/cauchy221/StyleTunedLM) framework. All generated paragraphs and ground-truth paragraphs are split into sentences, then three feature sets are computed and compared:
   - **Lexical MSE**: Mean squared error between 6-dimensional feature vectors (avg nouns, verbs, adjectives, unique words, subjectivity, concrete words per sentence). Lower is better.
   - **Syntactic JSD**: Jensen-Shannon divergence between 5-category sentence type distributions (simple, compound, complex, complex-compound, other) computed via Stanza constituency parsing. Lower is better.
   - **Surface MSE**: Mean squared error between 5-dimensional feature vectors (avg commas, semicolons, colons, words per sentence, word length). Lower is better.

3. **Style Embedding Cosine Similarity** -- Cosine similarity between each generation's OpenAI embedding and the precomputed author mean embedding. Higher means the generated text is closer to the author's style space.

### Step 3: Quality Evaluation (`eval_quality.py`)

Two metrics are computed:

1. **GPT-4o LLM-as-Judge** -- Each generation is evaluated by GPT-4o (temperature=0) on two criteria (1-5 scale):
   - **Writing Quality**: Prose fluency, coherence, vocabulary, absence of degenerate repetition.
   - **Task-Specific Quality**: For continuation -- does the text naturally follow the context? For summary -- does the text follow the plot description?

   **Continuation task prompt template:**

   ```
   You are an expert literary critic evaluating AI-generated creative writing.
   You are evaluating a paragraph generated by a model that was asked to continue
   writing from a given context paragraph in the style of {author}.

   **Context paragraph:**
   {context}

   **Generated continuation:**
   {generation}

   Evaluate the generated continuation on the following two criteria, each on a
   scale of 1-5:

   1. **Writing Quality**: Is the prose fluent and smooth? Are sentences
      well-constructed with appropriate word choice and varied vocabulary? Is it
      internally coherent (no contradictions, no nonsensical passages, no
      degenerate repetition)?
      1 = Incoherent, repetitive, or nonsensical
      2 = Mostly readable but with notable awkwardness, errors, or unnatural phrasing
      3 = Competent writing with minor issues in fluency or word choice
      4 = Good writing — smooth prose, natural phrasing, appropriate vocabulary
      5 = Excellent, polished writing that reads naturally throughout

   2. **Continuation Quality**: Does the generated text naturally follow from the
      context paragraph? Does it maintain consistent characters, setting, tone,
      and narrative direction? Does it feel like a plausible next paragraph in the
      story?
      1 = Completely disconnected from the context
      2 = Loosely related but with major inconsistencies in characters, setting, or tone
      3 = Reasonable continuation with some inconsistencies
      4 = Natural continuation that maintains most narrative elements
      5 = Seamless continuation that perfectly extends the context

   Respond ONLY with valid JSON (no markdown, no extra text):
   {"writing_quality": <int 1-5>, "continuation_quality": <int 1-5>,
    "justification": "<brief 1-2 sentence explanation>"}
   ```

   **Summary task prompt template:**

   ```
   You are an expert literary critic evaluating AI-generated creative writing.
   You are evaluating a paragraph generated by a model that was asked to write a
   paragraph based on a content description in the style of {author}.

   **Content description:**
   {summary}

   **Generated paragraph:**
   {generation}

   Evaluate the generated paragraph on the following two criteria, each on a
   scale of 1-5:

   1. **Writing Quality**: Is the prose fluent and smooth? Are sentences
      well-constructed with appropriate word choice and varied vocabulary? Is it
      internally coherent (no contradictions, no nonsensical passages, no
      degenerate repetition)?
      1 = Incoherent, repetitive, or nonsensical
      2 = Mostly readable but with notable awkwardness, errors, or unnatural phrasing
      3 = Competent writing with minor issues in fluency or word choice
      4 = Good writing — smooth prose, natural phrasing, appropriate vocabulary
      5 = Excellent, polished writing that reads naturally throughout

   2. **Plot Adherence**: Does the generated text faithfully follow the content
      described in the description? Does it cover the key events, characters, and
      details mentioned?
      1 = Completely ignores the content description
      2 = Loosely related but misses most key points
      3 = Covers some key points but misses others
      4 = Covers most key points with minor omissions
      5 = Thoroughly and faithfully follows the content description

   Respond ONLY with valid JSON (no markdown, no extra text):
   {"writing_quality": <int 1-5>, "plot_adherence": <int 1-5>,
    "justification": "<brief 1-2 sentence explanation>"}
   ```

2. **Quality Perplexity** -- Perplexity of generated text under `meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B` (a third-party model unrelated to training). Lower perplexity indicates more fluent, natural text. This provides an automated fluency signal independent of the finetuning model.

## Analysis

### Finetuning consistently improves quality and style capture

Across all 4 author/task combinations, finetuned models show:

- **Higher writing quality** (+0.48 to +1.23 on GPT-4o's 5-point scale). The improvement is larger for continuation tasks (~+1.2) than summary tasks (~+0.5), likely because baseline models struggle more with open-ended generation.
- **Higher task-specific quality** (+0.55 to +1.25). Finetuned models produce more coherent continuations and more faithful plot-based paragraphs.
- **Much lower quality perplexity** (2-3.5x reduction under Llama-3.1-8B), confirming the generated text is more fluent and natural.
- **Lower style perplexity** on held-out book text, meaning the finetuned model better predicts the author's actual writing patterns.
- **Much higher style embedding similarity** (+0.22 to +0.32), with lower variance, indicating finetuned generations are consistently closer to the author's style space.
- **Better syntactic alignment** in all cases (lower JSD), meaning finetuned models match the author's sentence structure distribution.

### Linguistic alignment is mixed for Stewart O'Nan

For Jason Reynolds, finetuning improves all three linguistic alignment metrics (lexical, syntactic, surface). However, for Stewart O'Nan, lexical MSE and surface MSE are *worse* for finetuned models despite strong improvements on all other metrics.

This suggests the finetuned model captures Stewart O'Nan's overall stylistic signature (high cosine similarity, low syntactic JSD) but overshoots on specific surface-level features such as sentence length and vocabulary usage patterns. This may be related to occasional degenerate outputs (repetitive or overly short generations) observed in the finetuned model's outputs, which would disproportionately affect surface and lexical feature averages.

### Continuation task benefits more from finetuning

The improvement gap between finetuned and baseline is consistently larger for continuation than summary. Baseline models score ~2.2-2.3 on continuation quality but ~3.1-3.4 on summary quality, suggesting that the structured input of summary (explicit plot description) partially compensates for the lack of author-specific finetuning. Open-ended continuation, which requires the model to independently maintain narrative coherence and style, benefits substantially more from finetuning.
